Today we will start discussing orthogonality (i.e.perpendicularity). First of all, we know that
two vectors v, w ∈Rn are orthogonal precisely when their dot product is 0:
v ⊥w
⇔
v · w = vT w = 0
(88)
If two vectors v and w are orthogonal, then we have the Pythagorean theorem:
||v + w||2 = ||v||2 + ||w||2
where we recall formula (7) for the length ||v|| of a vector (proof of the Pythagorean theorem:
||v + w||2 = (v + w) · (v + w) = v · v + v · w + w · v + w · w = ||v||2 + ||w||2).
But now that we have the necessary language to talk about more than just individual vectors in
Rn, we can generalize the concept of orthogonality to entire subspaces:
Deﬁnition 11. Two subspaces V, W ⊂Rn are called orthogonal, which will be denoted by V ⊥W,
if we have v ⊥w for any two vectors v ∈V and w ∈W.
Note that two subspaces can be orthogonal only if dim V + dim W ≤n. Otherwise, the two sub-
spaces would intersect in a non-zero vector v ∈V ∩W (think about two planes passing through
the origin in R3: they always contain a whole line) and it would be impossible for v ⊥v.

The fundamental theorem of linear algebra says that the four fundamental subspaces of a matrix
are pairwise orthogonal complements. Here’s why we like this setup: if you have two complemetary
subspaces V, W ⊂Rn (as deﬁned in Lecture 10) then any vector a ∈Rn can be written as:
a = v + w
where v ∈V and w ∈W
(94)
Moreover, v and w with this property are unique, and they are called the components of the
vector a in the complementary subspaces V and W, respectively. So for example, letting V and W
be the nullspace and row space of an m × n matrix, then we conclude that any vector in Rn can
be written uniquely as a linear combination of the rows of A plus a vector annihilated by A.
In the special case when V, W are complementary orthogonal subspaces of Rn, i.e.:
V = W ⊥
or, equivalently
W = V ⊥
the decomposition (94) takes on a more geometric meaning. In this case, v and w are none other
than the projections of the general vector a onto the subspaces V and W:
v = projV a
and
w = projW a
We conclude that any vector is the sum of its projections onto complementary orthogonal subspaces.
