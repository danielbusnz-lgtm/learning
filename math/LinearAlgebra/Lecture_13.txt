A big computational application of projections is least squares approximation, which we will
now delve into. Mathematically, the problem involves a system of equations for given A and b:
Av = b
(107)
which does not have any solutions, because the number of equations is greater than the number of
variables (mathematically, think of A as an m × n matrix with m > n). Therefore, the task is to
ﬁnd a value of v which makes Av as close as possible to b. In other words, the error vector:
e = b −Av
should be made as “small” as possible. Since we are dealing with vectors, “small” means that the
length of the vector should be as small as possible. Therefore, the problem is to ﬁnd v such that:
||e|| = ||b −Av||
(108)
is as small as possible, for given A and b. But what does this mean geometrically? As v ranges
over all possible vectors, Av ranges over the column space C(A). If b were already in C(A), then
the equation (107) could be satisﬁed with no error. But in general, b /∈C(A), in which case:
||b −Av|| ≥

distance from b to the subspace C(A)

= ||b −p||
where p is the orthogonal projection of b onto the subspace C(A). Therefore, ﬁnding v which
minimizes the quantity (108) actually involves two steps:
• ﬁnd the orthogonal projection p of b onto the subspace C(A)
• solve the equation Av = p (which can be done, since p ∈C(A))
We already know the answer to the ﬁrst question from (102):
p = A(AT A)−1AT b
The answer to the second question is also manifest, because a solution to the boxed equation is:
v = (AT A)−1AT b
(109)
Recall the fact (104): in order for the matrix AT A to be invertible and for the formula above to
make sense, we need the matrix A to have independent columns. This can always be arranged by
removing some dependent columns from the matrix A: for example, if the last column of A is a
linear combination of the other ones, then we can simply remove the last column of A and the last
entry of v, and the task of approximating equation (107) as well as possible remains the same.
