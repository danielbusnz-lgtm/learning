We will now put together the ideas of the previous two lectures in order to ﬁnd the complete
(a.k.a. general) solution v of the system:
Av = b
(63)
for any m × n matrix A. First of all, as we have seen in Fact 5, there are no solutions if the m × 1
vector b is not in the column space C(A). So we henceforth assume that b ∈C(A), which is just
saying that there exists a particular solution:
Avparticular = b
(64)
This n × 1 vector vparticular is a solution of (63), but it may not be the only one. In fact, for any
other solution v of the original system (63), we may subtract the two equations above and obtain:
A(v −vparticular) = 0
So the diﬀerence v −vparticular lies in the nullspace of A. This immediately leads to the following.
Fact 6. The general solution of the system (63) is given by:
vgeneral = vparticular + wgeneral
(65)
where:
• vparticular is a particular solution of (63), which exists if and only if b ∈C(A)
• wgeneral is any element of N(A), i.e. the general solution to Awgeneral = 0
The fact above is a general mathematical feature of linear equations. For example, it also holds in
the theory of linear ordinary diﬀerential equations, as you can see in 18.03.

Deﬁnition 7. The rank r of a matrix A is equal to the number of its pivot columns.
Here and throughout, the pivot columns of a matrix A refer to those columns where the pivots lie
in the (reduced) row echelon form of A.
If the matrix A is m × n, then the rank satiﬁes the inequality:
r ≤min(m, n)
since the number of pivots cannot surpass either the number of rows or of columns. We say that:
30
• A has full row rank if r = m. In this case, the column space of A is as big as possible, i.e.
C(A) = Rm, and the system Av = b has at least one solution v for any b.
• A has full column rank if r = n. In this case, the nullspace of A is as small as possible, i.e.
N(A) = 0, and the system Av = b has at most one solution v for any b.
