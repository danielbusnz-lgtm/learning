We will now give a rigorous deﬁnition of projections.
Deﬁnition 13. Given a vector b ∈Rn and a linear subspace V ⊂Rn, the (orthogonal) projec-
tion of b onto V is the unique vector:
p = projV b ∈V
such that (b −p) ⊥V . Put diﬀerently, p is the closest vector in V to b.
Intuitively, suppose you want to measure a quantity, which general theory tells you must be a vector
p in a subspace V ⊂Rn. However, your measurement will probably have an error, and you might
get a value b outside of V . Then your best guess for the actual value p ∈V is to set it equal to
the orthogonal projection of b on V , since this is the closest vector in V to the measured value b.
That is also why the vector e = b −p is sometimes called the error.

Fact 9. For any subspace V ⊂Rn, there exists an n × n matrix PV such that:
projV b = PV b
(99)
for any vector b ∈Rn.
So all orthogonal projections are given by left multiplication with a suitable matrix (unoriginally
called a “projection matrix”). The task for us now is to compute the matrix corresponding to a
given projection, and we will do it in the special case when:
V = C(A)
for an n×m matrix A. In fact, this does not represent any restriction on the subspace V , since any
subspace can be written as the column space of a suitably chosen matrix: just take basis vectors
of the subspace and put them next to each other in a matrix. So given b ∈Rn, we are looking for:
p ∈C(A)
such that
(b −p) ⊥C(A)

p = Av

AT (b −p) = 0
(101)

AT (b −Av) = 0
